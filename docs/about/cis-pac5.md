# About the programme — UNEP CIS-Pac5

This training course is delivered by **SPREP** as part of the **UNEP CIS-Pac5
programme**, funded by the **Green Climate Fund (GCF)** and implemented by the
**UN Environment Programme (UNEP)**. This page explains what that programme is
and where SPREP's component — and this workshop — fit within it.

## UNEP CIS-Pac5 at a glance

> **Enhancing Climate Information and Knowledge Services for resilience in 5
> island countries of the Pacific Ocean**

| | |
|---|---|
| GCF reference | **FP147** |
| Budget | **USD 49.9 million** |
| Approved | Green Climate Fund Board (B.27), 10 November 2020 |
| Implementing agency | UN Environment Programme (UNEP) |
| Five countries | Cook Islands · Niue · Palau · Republic of the Marshall Islands (RMI) · Tuvalu |

The programme establishes **integrated climate and ocean information services
and multi-hazard early warning systems (MHEWS)** across the five countries. It
responds to priority needs identified in the **Pacific Islands Meteorological
Strategy 2017–2026** and the **Framework for Resilient Development in the
Pacific**.

!!! info "Expected impact"
    - **At least 80%** of the populations of the five countries as direct
      beneficiaries.
    - A **15–30% reduction** in economic loss and damage from climate-related
      hazards.

## Why Pacific SIDS prioritise climate information

As Pacific Small Island Developing States (SIDS), the five Programme countries
are extremely vulnerable to increasingly frequent and intense climate hazards —
tropical cyclones, flooding, drought and sea-level rise. Disaster risk is at its
highest in Oceania: total damage-replacement costs for assets, infrastructure
and crops at risk across the five countries exceed **USD 7.6 billion**
(PCRAFI, 2019).

National Meteorological and Hydrological Services (NMHSs) underpin economic
growth and sustainable development, and early warning systems facilitate
disaster risk reduction and climate-change adaptation. Systematic investment in
climate services carries an estimated **cost–benefit ratio of about 1 : 10**
(WMO, 2019).

## What the programme does

- Facilitate a paradigm shift to **science-based, climate-resilient** policy,
  planning, preparedness and early action.
- Strengthen delivery models for **climate information services and MHEWS**
  covering oceans and islands, including **National Frameworks for Climate
  Services**.
- Enable compliance with **Global Basic Observing Network (GBON)** standards for
  reliable surface observations.
- Strengthen **ocean services** for sustainable marine-ecosystem management,
  benefiting the coastal communities that make up 90–100% of the five countries'
  populations.
- Establish **impact-based forecasting and forecast-based financing**.
- Enhance **people-centred, last-mile** dissemination of risk information and
  early warnings — tailored for women, youth, elderly people, Indigenous Peoples
  and people with disabilities.
- Improve preparedness and **community-led disaster risk management**,
  integrating Traditional Knowledge.
- **Enhance regional knowledge management and cooperation** for climate services
  and MHEWS.

## SPREP's component — regional knowledge management & capacity building

The **Secretariat of the Pacific Regional Environment Programme (SPREP)** is one
of the programme's regional partners. SPREP's contribution centres on the
programme's **regional knowledge-management, coordination and capacity-building**
activities — helping Pacific climate, GIS and data officers turn global climate
and ocean data into decision-ready information for their own countries, and
sharing that capability across the region to avoid duplication and build
economies of scale.

**This course is part of that component.** *Regional Capacity Building on GIS &
Data Management* builds the practical skills that sit behind every climate
information service and early-warning product:

- **finding and accessing** global climate and ocean datasets;
- **processing and localising** them to a specific country or island;
- **mapping, charting and exporting** results for use in national systems
  (QGIS, ArcGIS) — using free, browser-based tools such as Google Earth Engine.

By strengthening these data skills regionally, the workshop directly supports
the programme's goal of stronger, locally-owned climate information services —
and equips officers in SPREP member countries beyond the five FP147 nations to
do the same work for their own contexts.

## Global frameworks the programme advances

The Programme contributes directly to **Article 7 of the Paris Agreement**,
**Sustainable Development Goal 13 (Climate Action)**, the **Sendai Framework for
Disaster Risk Reduction**, the **SAMOA Pathway**, and the **UN Decade of Ocean
Science for Sustainable Development**.
